The mirror was old — a silver lid with engraving, worn from a thousand touches. Grandmother's. The only thing Lena had taken with her when she left Vladivostok eight years ago.

She looked at her reflection and thought about the fact that she was twenty-six, that she had already sat through this shift for nine hours, and that the coffee in the embassy cafeteria was getting worse with each passing year.

The telephone rang. Internal line. A call redirected from the main switchboard. Lena snapped the mirror shut, set it aside, and answered the call.

"Embassy of the Russian Federation, my name is Elena, please introduce yourself."

The voice was automatic, rehearsed through thousands of such calls. Lost passports. Visa questions. Sometimes — hysterics from tourists stranded at the airport.

A second of silence.

Lena had already opened her mouth to repeat the greeting when a female voice uttered a single word:

"Vympel."

The world stopped.

Lena felt a chill run down her spine — not fear, no. Something else. Something she had been taught three years ago, in a room without windows, by instructors without names.

"This word you will hear, perhaps, never. But if you do hear it — everything else ceases to exist."

Her fingers were already moving across the keyboard — quickly, precisely, without a single unnecessary command. Activation protocol. Voice identification. Biometric analysis in real time.

A face appeared on the screen.

A woman. Forty-something. Blonde — natural, this was evident from the texture, from the way light fell on the hair. Slender, fit, with the posture of a person accustomed to command. Sculptural cheekbones. Lips that probably knew how to smile — but not now.

And the eyes.

Blue. Piercing. Eyes that had seen something terrible and had not looked away.

"This woman sounds as if she is ready to give her life for her children right now," thought Lena.

She lowered her gaze below — to where the system had already displayed the dossier.

GOLITSYNA, Jane. née Smith.
Status: PROTECTED.
Linked file: GOLITSYN, Mark. Callsign: PORUCHIK.
Status: KIA. Operation [REDACTED]. Date: [REDACTED].

Lena stared at the screen.

"Just as her husband gave his for someone else's..."

She closed her eyes for a second. One second — an impermissible luxury. Then opened them and switched to English.

"The code, please."

The voice on the line did not waver:

"Seven. Four. Two. Nine. Zero. Three. Eight. One."

Lena entered the digits. The system flashed green.

CONFIRMED. EXTRACTION PROTOCOL ALPHA. PRIORITY: MAXIMUM.

Her fingers were already typing commands — one after another, activating a chain that would now awaken people at three different points in the city. People who had been waiting for such a call at every moment. People who, perhaps, had hoped it would never come.

"Mrs. Golitsyna," said Lena, and her own voice sounded different now. Firmer. Older. "Be ready in exactly twelve minutes."

She watched the timer in the corner of the screen. 12:00. 11:59. 11:58.

"There will be two cars. Sedan and minivan. Black. No markings."

11:52. 11:51.

"Get out of the house to the curb in eleven minutes. Take your sons. Take nothing else."

A pause on the other end. Brief. Almost imperceptible.

"When you reach the curb, tie the shoe of one of your sons."

11:41. 11:40.

"This is the signal. This tells our people that you are not under duress. That this is your choice."

Lena heard the breathing on the other end of the line. Even. Controlled. The breathing of a person who had already made a decision.

"Whatever happens — don't pay attention to anything. Cars, people, sounds. Nothing. Your only goal is to get inside of the minivan. The side door will be open."

11:22. 11:21.

"When the door closes behind you..."

Lena paused. Not for dramatic effect. Simply to make certain that the next words would be heard.

"...you are out of the jurisdiction. Forever."

Silence.

Then a voice — the voice of a woman with piercing blue eyes, a woman who had lost her husband and was now losing everything else in order to save her children:

"I understand."

A pause.

"Eleven minutes."

"Eleven minutes," confirmed Lena.

"Thank you, Elena."

The line disconnected.

Lena stared at the screen. The timer continued to run. 10:47. 10:46. 10:45.

In the photograph in the dossier, Jane Golitsyna was smiling. The picture was old — perhaps five years old. She stood on some veranda, flooded with sunlight, and looked somewhere beyond the frame. At someone.

"At him," Lena understood. "At the Poruchik."

She took out the mirror. Opened it. Looked at her reflection.

Twenty-six years old. Nine hours on shift. Bad coffee.

And a woman on the other end of the line who had ten minutes and forty-three seconds left to gather three children and step into the void.

Lena closed the mirror. She had done her job.

Now there was nothing left but to wait.
